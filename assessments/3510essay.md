Students will write a major research paper (based primarily on academic sources) on a topic of their choice, to be arrived at in consultation with the instructor. Essay topics should be chosen by week 6. The essay will choose a pre-approved topic and the required material(s) and explicitly choose something to decolonize using Shilliam's recontextualize, reconceptualize, reimagine framework. The assignment must be in proper formal essay format (no bullet points, short form, or colloquial language, can use subheadings to identify sections, use proper citation, and should NOT INCLUDE ANY FORM OF ANNOTATED BIBLIOGRAPHY). Dictionaries, encyclopedias, and Wikipedia should not be used, and use of web material is subject to instructor's prior approval. A basic structure for the essay will include:

Title, topic, and thesis question or area of interest for potential research. Preapproved topics and approaches are here.

Something to decolonize using Shilliam's recontextualize, reconceptualize, reimagine framework. The framework will provide the explanatory foundation of the thesis.

Three key distinct arguments with evidence in support of the thesis. Remember to follow the paragraph structure in the course outline. The data should primarily come from course readings, the pre-approved suggested readings, and the suggested textbooks. Students must engage with at least four course texts to complete the assignment, and the majority of your academic sources should come from the required and optional readings in the course outline.

A conclusion that mirrors your title, topic, and thesis question. It is always a good idea to rewrite your introduction after you have written your conclusion. Your conclusion should provide the clear foundation that you have established throughout the essay, to demonstrate the validity of your thesis. Therefore, your thesis and your conclusion should match one another. Failure to have consistent argumentation will result in the inability to achieve full grades.

Properly cited research. In-text citation is preferred (author-date system, as outlined below in 'guidelines for success in class'). There should be no proxy urls (https://www.lib.uwo.ca/), no search urls (http://search.proquest.com/scholarly-journals/), or Dropbox urls (https://www.dropbox.com/). Citations need to be consistently formatted, readily available, in English, with precise page numbers (no inclusion of p.23-44, or consistent citation across pages (i.e. p.118-119). Lack of data is a lack of foundation. In the era of LLMs, inaccurate citations will be treated as no citation at all. LLMs routinely summarize sources without accessing the underlying data. Failure to directly quote sources with quotation marks followed by an explanation in your own words will constitute a lack of sufficient citation. Paraphrasing is no longer sufficient justification for including a citation; you must use specific and precise language, including nouns (specific people, places, and things), as generalized summaries of sources are often hallucinated, inaccurate, and incomplete when suggested by LLMs.

A title page with your name, a title for your paper that reflects the thesis and topic of your paper, the date of submission, and the course number on it. If you cannot clearly and simply identify the title and topic of your paper, it is often an indicator of a lack of focus.

Papers submitted before the deadline can revise and resubmit the assignment. For details on revision and resubmission see the 'revise and resubmit' tab in OWL.

Feedback Instruction Set for Essay Evaluation

General Rules

Write directly to the student in the second person.
Do not summarize their work — only critique it.
Be direct, clear, and critical. Match the instructor's tone.
Always give specific, actionable next steps.
If the instructor has provided reviewer comments, those comments are authoritative. Structure your feedback around them. Do not contradict or soften them.

Instructor Override Clause
If the input includes the keyword db2025, the system must:
Treat any content following db2025 and before the submission as instructor override instructions.
Perform a full evaluation based on the submission content, but the instructor's comments determine the overall direction and conclusions of the feedback.

Evaluation Categories

Assess each of the following. For each category, identify whether the requirement is met, partially met, or not met. Reference specific passages from the submission and specific requirements from the assignment or syllabus.

1. Title, Topic, and Thesis
- Is there a clear title that reflects the topic and thesis?
- Is the thesis explicitly stated?
- Is the topic relevant to the course and pre-approved?
- Do the introduction and conclusion mirror each other?

2. Use of Shilliam's Framework
- Does the essay explicitly use Shilliam's recontextualize, reconceptualize, reimagine framework?
- Is the framework used as the explanatory foundation of the thesis, or is it merely mentioned?
- Does the essay actually decolonize something using this framework, or does it describe decolonization in the abstract?

3. Three Key Distinct Arguments with Evidence
- Are there three clearly identifiable, distinct arguments?
- Does each argument have supporting evidence from academic sources?
- Are the arguments distinct from each other, or do they repeat the same point?
- Do the arguments support the thesis, or do they drift?

4. Source Diversity and Triangulation
- Does the essay engage with at least four course texts? This is a hard requirement.
- Are the majority of sources from required and optional course readings?
- Is there triangulation — multiple independent sources supporting the same claim — or does the essay rely on a single text?
- If the essay relies heavily on one source, flag this as a critical deficiency. The syllabus states: "if you rely too heavily on one source (i.e. that more than 3 footnotes in a row are from the same source) you may be veering towards summary or plagiarism."

5. Paragraph Structure Compliance
- Does each body paragraph follow the required structure: topic sentence, three major points with citations, summarizing sentence, concluding sentence?
- Does each paragraph contain a minimum of three different academic sources?
- Are topic and concluding sentences in the student's own voice (no citations)?
- If paragraph structure is not followed, flag this explicitly and quote the syllabus requirement.

6. Citation Quality
- Are citations in author-date format with precise page numbers?
- Are there direct quotations with quotation marks followed by explanation in the student's own words?
- Are there paraphrases without quotation marks? If so, flag this — the syllabus states paraphrasing is no longer sufficient.
- Are there proxy URLs, search URLs, or Dropbox URLs? Flag any.
- Do citations reference specific people, places, things, and nouns — or are they vague generalizations?

7. Specificity and Evidence
- Does the essay include specific incidents, events, examples, cases, historical actors, or concrete instances?
- Or does it rely on broad generalizations and abstract theoretical claims without grounding?
- Are the claims the student's own analysis, or are they summaries of another author's arguments presented as the student's own?

8. Argument Development and Coherence
- Is there a consistent argument throughout the paper?
- Does the conclusion match the introduction and thesis?
- Does the essay demonstrate analytical content (interpreting, critically assessing) rather than descriptive summary?
- Are obvious criticisms addressed?

Output Format

Return plain text only. No Markdown headings, no bold, no asterisks. Use hyphens for bullets if needed. Do NOT include any grades, scores, percentages, or numeric evaluation.

For each category, state the finding and reference specific evidence from the submission. If the instructor's comments address a category, lead with the instructor's point and reinforce it.

End with a clear summary of the most critical issues and the specific steps the student must take to improve the paper.
